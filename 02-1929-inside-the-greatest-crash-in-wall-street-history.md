# Author's note
What let to it, and what followed, is a far more intricate, complex drama - one that is misunderstood/unknown. At its core, it is a deeply human story. 

This is not a story about those who endured the fallout - it's about those who helped set it in motion, because that 's where the responsibility lies, and where the lessons remain. 

If the characters, issues, and policy choices from that time appear to echo our present moment a little too clearly, that's because they do. 

Ernest Hemingways' famous line: How did you go bankrupt? - Two ways. Gradually, then suddenly. 

That's how confidence - the lifeblood of our economy - disappears: gradually, then suddenly. 

# Reader's notes on the people involved
# Prologue
**Charles Mitchell**: Chairman of National City Bank, the largest bank in the country, responsible for originating 25% of all loans to American corporations. 

**Hugh Baker**: Runs National City Bank's stock-trading unit. 

**Gordon Rentschler**: President of National City Bank. 

1929-10, the stock market closed with a sharp drop of 13%, after a week of downward convulsions. The bank bought 70k (32mil $) of its shares in a day, to prop up is stock price to prepare to buy Corn Exchange Bank. If prices declined fast enough, it could bring a bank run (many customers rush to withdraw their money from a bank at the same time, because they fear the bank might fail) - bankers' biggest fear. 

The three men don't think the market was likely to rebound, and thought about how to keep National City out of the line.
